**Synopsis - The Wrong Kind of Immortality**

Orestis Stathis became immortal by accident—and spent a thousand years learning why that was a curse, not a gift.

His body could no longer be harmed. It could no longer feel pleasure either. With nothing at stake and nothing to gain, life slowly hollowed out.

After centuries of trying to die, he wakes up in his own childhood, years before the immortality ever took hold. Now, he has one goal: live quietly, avoid notice, and die properly this time.

It should be simple. He knows more about magic, gods, and broken systems than anyone alive. He has a thousand years of experience hiding in plain sight. All he needs to do is keep his head down and let events unfold without him.

But knowledge is hard to contain, and competence even harder to hide. One offhand correction becomes a reputation. A quiet analysis becomes a career. A subtle act of spite against the institution that destroyed his family becomes the first move in a war he never intended to fight.

And then there's Eirene—a merchant's daughter whose intelligence and stubbornness make her impossible to dismiss, inconvenient to care about, and dangerous to underestimate.

He wanted a quiet life. He's getting something considerably more complicated.

**What to Expect**

This is a character-driven progression story focused on systems, institutions, and long-term strategy rather than direct power scaling.

Orestis is not climbing ranks, collecting abilities, or punching progressively larger enemies. He's an exhausted, sardonic man with a thousand years of knowledge, operating inside institutions—a magical consortium, trade networks, political systems—and using those systems as weapons.

The story (currently) focuses on: institutional politics and quiet sabotage; a magic system treated as engineering; a protagonist who is genuinely competent without being infallible; a slow-burn relationship between two people who communicate in seven words what most people need seven paragraphs for; war strategy where logistics matter more than fireballs; and a god who finds the whole situation entertaining.

The tone is dry, direct, and character-driven. If you enjoy protagonists who solve problems through documentation, footnotes, and carefully adjusted terminology, this is probably your kind of story.

Oh, and expect the occasional mountain-shearing, landscape-rearranging battle.

**Where the story is now:** 55 chapters published (including side chapters), updating Mon/Wed/Fri. The story has moved through an institutional arc, a war arc, and has just entered a new continent arc. The scope keeps expanding—but the voice stays the same.

**Honest caveats:** The pacing in the middle section (ch17–24) was originally slower than it should have been. I rewrote those chapters based on reader feedback—same plot points, better pacing, more character moments. The story finds its rhythm around ch26, according to most of the reader feedback.
